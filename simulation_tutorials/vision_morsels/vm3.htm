Morsel 3: The Point of View Example

Seeing how the world looks from another person's point of view can be a powerful experience. If we assume that experience drives one's perception of the world, then a typical member of group A would probably have the same attitudes as a typical member of group B, if only that person had been born into group B.

The world is filled with groups of people that do not understand each other. Here we will be using the generic terms of 'group A' and 'group B,' but feel free to switch this in your mind to whichever two groups you like (Israeli and Palestinian, Farmer and Cattle-herder, Business Person and Environmentalist, etc.)

 

The Goal

The goal of this online simulation is to give members of two groups a deeper understanding of what it is like to be a member of the other group.

Assumptions

It is assumed that the simulation is conducted online, so the players will not need to meet each other - the two players may come from groups with a great deal of animosity between them.

It is also assumed that the players have some material reason for playing. They may be doing it for credit, they may be doing it for preparation for negotiation, they may even be doing it for money (the small amounts like those given for typical psychological experiments). It is not assumed that they are doing it for fun. Seeing the world from another person's point of view frequently requires a lot of mental work - and is not fun.

First Step: Defining the Two Worlds

The simulation creator(s) will have to research and understand thoroughly what it feels like to be a member of either group. Materials will have to be developed that explain what it feels like to be a member coming from the other group, and how their world is perceived. In fact, it will probably be best if this material comes from two simulation creators, one from group A and one from group B.

In this simulation we envision there being two maps. Both will convey the same physical terrain: the basic shape of the country will be the same. But on top of the regular terrain will be indicated the memories of the Actor being played. For example, on the map of the world as seen by a member of group A, there may be the following additional 'emotional landmarks':

  • Place where members of group B beat my grandfather.
  • Place where members of group B destroyed my school.
  • Group B Checkpoint where my my friend Tony's father disappeared.

The map of the world for group B would contain similar types of landmarks.

Second Step: Give Them Something to Discuss

The next step will be to create a focus of discussion for the two Actors to talk about. It should be a small item, not the entire point of disagreement between the two peoples, but may be a microcosm of the larger issues.

Defining Phases and Sections

Once the above materials have been generated, using the USIP OSCW to create the simulation will be easy. For this simulation there will be three parties involved: Actor A, Actor B and Control. Fictional Actor A will come from group A (and will be played by a real live person from group B.) Fictional Actor B will come from group B. The Control actor will be an impartial instructor.

In the diagram below we show the simulation sections available to the three actors in the two phases of the simulation. Essentially in this simulation all that Actor A and B can do is read about their world and chat with each other. The Control actor can also chat (but probably will not) and can write the after action report and change the simulation phase from 'in progress' to 'done' - effectively terminating the simulation.

 

Conducting the Simulation

As previously indicated, when this simulation is conducted a member of group B will play the role of the fictional Actor coming from group A, and vice a versa. Control will monitor their exchanges (this is a labor intensive simulation) and terminate it when the two characters have come some agreement or understanding on the small, fictional point of contention. Control will also be able to terminate the simulation if the exchanges between the two players grow too acrimonious.

Multiple sessions of this simulation can be used to help fine-tune the scenario, the introductory materials and the maps. Unfortunately, since this is such a simply constructed scenario - the players do not have any specific decisions to make - the automatic collection of data is limited. We will address ways to improve upon this in our next morsel.
