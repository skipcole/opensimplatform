USIP Online Simulation Creation Vision Morsels

     

 

Morsel 0: The Big "Why?

The Problem

The world we live in is complex. Many things affect many other things and the solutions to problems are frequently not obvious. Some people may know the solutions, or have a part of the solution in their mind, but the true answers are spread out amid a sea of noise. We need to find a better way to help good answers become apparent and to disseminate them in a form that sticks.

Our current methods of conveying lessons (reading, talking and role playing) are strong. They have been used for millenium. But they do run up against limitations. The human mind is quite fallible and groups of people quite susceptible to believing what they want to believe. We need methods that force people to face, or at least consider, real world constraints. Fortunately for us, technology is now making possible methods that include our traditional methods, and also incorporate hard, cold numerical facts.

The Solution

A numerical represantation of the world is called a 'simulation.' We are positing that allowing people to experience simulated, or virtual, worlds can be a fast and effective method to entrench lessons and build up the equivalent of real world experience. Even better, this experience is gained in a risk free environment. (Mess up in a real world hostage negotiation, and one can end up dead. Mess up in a virtual hostage negotiation, and one can end up embarrassed. We consider this 'risk free.')

Another hallmark of our times is that training is ceasing to be a one way form of communication. In a simpler world a group of people may have 'known' the answers. (They were frequently wrong, but lets let that pass.) But in today's world the reality is that 'we are smarter than me.' So we also want a tool that allows people to see all of the assumptions going into these simulations, and be able to provide meaningful feedback and possibly even build simulations on top of the work of others.

With this tool we attend to accomplish all of this.

Can't this all be done using some other tool, like 'Second Life'?

This tool, and the simulations created with it, emphasize strategic thinking and the importance of relationships. While much can be learned moving around in high fidelity 3D virtual worlds, many important lessons cannot be effectively taught there. One serious limitation of these worlds is that 1 minute = 1 minute. As Peter Senge points out in 'The Fifth Discipline' humans are not good at learning when the effect comes much later than the cause. Also, sometimes lessons simply just need to be learned faster. The world seldom gives one, for example, six months to prepare for a six-month tour.

Another limitation, although not as serious, is that these 3-D worlds do not usually present data in the boring old formats that strategic thinkers throughout the ages have relied on. Boring old maps and charts are still important ways to convey trends and help one make wise decisions. Little is gained by 'looking over the shoulder' of one's avatar at charts in a 3D world when those charts could just be simply displayed.

An 'invisible technology'

A technology becomes invisible when one doesn't even give it much consideration. For example, electricity is an 'invisible' technology in most parts of the world. We take for granted its availability. The ability to create simulations will also some day be an invisible technology without a lot of 'awe' associated with it. The sooner that day arrives, the better.

We would like to enable all people to be able to use a simple wizard to create multi-player online simulations. Currently this is still something that one needs a fairly large budget, and programming help to do. The number of experts involved in creating simulations is still a rather small set of people. By enabling the subject matter experts direct access to putting their ideas in this new medium, we will be able to draw out the wisdom from far more people, and will be able to take the playing field to a higher level.

Our final trump card

There are other advantages to this system, but let us end with what is certainly our strongest argument: this tool is open source. As such it can always be modified by anyone who needs it, and it can easily incorporate any of the great new open source products being made available every day.
